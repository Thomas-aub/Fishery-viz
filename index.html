Mapping Madagascar's Artisanal Fisheries

In Madagascar, small-scale fisheries represent a vital socio-economic and nutritional pillar for coastal communities, yet this sector remains largely invisible in official statistics, with only 11% of traditional fishing canoes officially registered. This lack of data hinders effective environmental management and the evaluation of Marine Protected Areas.

To overcome this blind spot, a research study conducted at the MARBEC laboratory by Thomas Aubourg under the direction of David Mouillot demonstrates the potential of combining satellite imagery and deep learning. By leveraging premium Pléiades Neo imagery at 0.30 m/pixel for manual annotation, an optimized YOLO26 model using oriented bounding boxes was trained to capture the faint visual signatures of these tiny non-motorized wooden canoes.

Furthermore, a physics-based sensor degradation pipeline was established to transfer these insights down to standard Pléiades imagery at 0.50 m/pixel, enabling cost-effective operational deployment. The optimized model achieves an encouraging mAP50 of 71.8% and an F1-score of 70.3% on the validation test corpus, laying the methodological foundation for national-scale mapping and sustainable coastal governance.
