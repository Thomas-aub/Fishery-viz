An invisible fleet

Along Madagascar's coasts, tens of thousands of wooden pirogues leave shore before dawn every day. They carry most of the country's small-scale catch ‐ yet almost none of them appear in any official registry.

Fewer than 11% of Malagasy fishing canoes are formally registered, leaving fisheries managers, marine planners, and the blue economy agenda without a reliable picture of where fishing effort actually happens.

Pléiades Neo · 0.30 m/px

Seeing what isn't reported

To close this gap, we turn to Very High Resolution satellite imagery from the Pléiades Neo constellation, sharp enough to resolve individual pirogues at 0.30 m per pixel.

A custom YOLO26 oriented-bounding-box detection pipeline scans each scene to locate and count vessels automatically, converting raw imagery into a density map of fishing activity ‐ box by box, kilometre by kilometre.

Zoom target · Antsiranana

Hotspots in the North

Density is far from uniform. Around Antsiranana (Diego Suarez), sheltered bays and a historic fishing culture concentrate pirogue traffic into some of the highest densities measured anywhere on the coast.

These clusters point to where governance and monitoring resources would have the greatest impact first.

Zoom target · Mahajanga

A second cluster: Mahajanga

On the northwest coast, the port city of Mahajanga anchors another dense concentration of activity, shaped by the wide estuary of the Betsiboka River and easy access to productive coastal waters.

Comparing hotspots like these across the coastline is the first step toward a national, satellite-derived map of artisanal fishing effort.
